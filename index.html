ADENOSINE

This package intends to concisely describe adenosine and its use in supraventricular tachycardias, including method of administration 

Indications and Uses

  1. Primary use  
    Diagnostic/Therapeutic use in SVTs (supraventricular tachycardia) 
  2. Less common uses 
    Rarely in myocardial perfusion stress imaging (as a vasodilator) 

MECHANISM OF ACTION

Acts on adenosine receptors
A1, A2a, A2B, and A3 

Clinical manifestation
receptors found in the atrioventricular (AV) node, as well as in the peripheral vasculature
Acts as a miscellaneous antiarrythmic (not characterised by Vaughan-Williams) 
 - activates specific potassium channels 
 - inhibits calcium influx, disrupting the resting potential of the slow nodal
 - cardiac myocyte. 
 - thereby decreases conduction velocity through the AV node 
causing a transient AV block 
Duration of action is very short due to rapid metabolism: Half life <10 seconds

USE IN SUPRAVENTRICULAR TACHYCARDIAS (SVT)

  • Supraventricular tachycardia: Defined as any arrhythmia originating at or above the AV node (therefore narrow-complex + >100bpm)
    Definition usually excludes Atrial Fibrillation
    Due to fast rate, can be difficult to differentiate from each other 
  • Uses of adenosine: 
    is able to slow the ventricular rate for a short time to allow for diagnosis
    may be able to terminate AVRT + AVNRT 

ADVERSE EFFECTS

Most side effects should however be very transient oweing to rapid metabolism of adenosine; management is supportive only

  1. Potent vasodilator
     - skin flushing; lightheadedness; nausea; sweating; nervousness  
     - feeling of impending doom 
  2. Cardiac-related
     - development of dangerous arrythmia in patient with accessory pathways, due to uncontrolled conduction via accessory pathway (e.g. WPW or broad-compex tachycardia; therefore contra-indicated)  
     - cardiac ischaemia  
     - hypotension  
     - prolonged asystole 
  3. Respiratory
     - bronchospasm (therefore contra-indicated in asthma/COPD) 

ADMINISTRATION

IV (via peripheral cannuale or central line)
Can be given via 3-way tap (see animation)
1. connect adenosine and flush to the 3-way tap
2. ensure the ‘close’ handle is positioned over the flush, so as to not mix the adenosine into the flush syringe
3. inject the adenosine (6mg) rapidly
4. reposition the ‘close’ handle over the empty adenosine syringe, so not to push the flush into the empty syringe
5. followed by 10ml flush rapidly
6. subsequent 12mg doses can be given if no/limited effect

Should be on continuous cardiac monitoring, either:
 - a printing 12-lead ECG machine (press rhythm button to continuously print)
 - a printing defibrillator found on a crash-trolley

Usually initiated by seniors/cardiology if lack of experience  



Note: different types of 3-way 'tap' or 'stopcock'
